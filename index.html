Analyzing SPR Data with No Observable Dissociation

Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) instruments are used to study molecular interactions by measuring the association and subsequent dissociation of the molecules. Data may exhibit a flat dissociation phase when molecules bind with high affinity. Kinetic analysis software frequently attempts to fit a mathematical model to this flat section. This results in the quantification of baseline instrument noise rather than the true biological interaction. Best practices dictate defining the lowest measurable limit of the instrument instead of reporting non-physical absolute values.

A minimum signal decay of 5% is generally required to confirm an observable dissociation event. If the signal decays by less than 5% over the observed window, the true dissociation rate ($k_d$) cannot be accurately determined. An observable limit should be calculated for these scenarios.

1. The Limit Calculator

The minimum observable dissociation rate ($k_{d\_min}$) depends on the duration of the dissociation phase ($t_d$). This utilizes a first-order exponential decay relationship.

$$k_{d\_min}=\frac{-\ln(0.95)}{t_d}$$

Reporting Protocols

It is recommended practice to report flat dissociation data as limits rather than distinct numerical values. The $k_d$ is recorded as an inequality. This denotes it is less than the calculated minimum observable limit.

The equilibrium dissociation constant ($K_D$) represents the ratio of the off-rate to the on-rate ($k_a$).

$$K_D=\frac{k_d}{k_a}$$

Calculate the $K_D$ limit using the $k_d$ limit and the measured $k_a$. The experimental methodology section should explicitly state that the minimum dissociation rate was determined utilizing a 5% signal loss threshold over the measured dissociation duration.

Reporting Example:

If the mathematical fit yields a non-physical $k_d$ of $1.00 \times 10^{-6}$ s$^{-1}$ for a 600s dissociation phase, do not report it. Instead, utilize the calculated limit ($8.5 \times 10^{-5}$ s$^{-1}$). Assuming an accurately measured $k_a$ of $9.16 \times 10^{4}$ M$^{-1}$s$^{-1}$, the final reported parameters should look like this:

$$k_a = 9.16 \times 10^{4} \text{ M}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}$$ $$k_d < 8.50 \times 10^{-5} \text{ s}^{-1}$$ $$K_D < 9.28 \times 10^{-10} \text{ M}$$

Handling Replicates and Baseline Variability

When evaluating multiple replicates, slight variations in the baseline can occur. This is a common aspect of instrument performance. However, this variability can complicate analysis if the algorithm attempts to fit these minor fluctuations as true dissociation events. Ensure proper double-referencing is applied to remove systematic background noise. Buffer Blanks are available on SW ver 2.6.0+ to assist with this subtraction.

If the sensorgrams remain largely flat but show minor differences, do not average the separate $k_d$ values generated by the software. Evaluate the total signal change over the dissociation period for each replicate relative to the 5% threshold. Identifying and Resolving Experimental Artifacts

A flat dissociation phase does not always indicate a high-affinity interaction. Experimental artifacts can trap the analyte on the sensor surface, artificially simulating a slow dissociation rate. Recognizing these artifacts is critical for accurate data interpretation. Common experimental factors include:

  • Mass Transport Limitation (Rebinding): High ligand density ($R_{max}$) prevents the analyte from diffusing away into the bulk flow. Upon dissociation, the analyte immediately rebinds to an adjacent ligand. Fix this by immobilizing less ligand to achieve a lower capacity.
  • Avidity Effects: Multivalent analytes (e.g., bivalent antibodies) bind to multiple target sites simultaneously on a densely packed surface. The probability of all binding sites dissociating simultaneously is extremely low, resulting in a flat response. Fix this by inverting the assay orientation. Immobilize the multivalent molecule and flow the monomeric target as the analyte.
  • Non-Specific Binding (NSB): Analytes may interact directly with the sensor matrix due to electrostatic or hydrophobic forces, independent of the target ligand. Standard running buffers already contain 0.1% Tween-20, which may not be enough. Fix this by monitoring the reference channel for NSB and adjusting the running buffer. Increase the salt concentration (e.g., 300-500 mM NaCl), or add blocking agents like BSA (1 mg/mL) or soluble dextran (1 mg/mL) to disrupt these secondary interactions.
  • Analyte Aggregation: Poor sample stability can lead to protein aggregation on the sensor surface. This mass accumulates and does not dissociate. Fix this by centrifuging samples immediately prior to analysis and adjusting buffer conditions to maintain protein solubility.

Implementation in the Nicosystem

Here is how you can apply these concepts directly in your analysis software to ensure your results reflect physical reality.

Method A: Setting a Limit Boundary

You can force the software to recognize the physical limitation of your experiment by setting the parameter manually.

  1. Go to the Model and Accommodation section.
  2. Set the $k_d$ parameter to constant.
  3. Enter the $k_{d\_min}$ limit value you calculated based on your dissociation time.
  4. Select Apply Locally to run the final fit with this boundary locked in place.

Method B: Fitting the Off-Rate First

Sometimes, the algorithm struggles to fit the entire sensorgram when the off-rate is non-uniform, leading to poor $k_a$ values. You can guide the algorithm by isolating the dissociation phase first.

  1. Adjust your Model and Accomodation model to the Off Rate Model.
  2. Fit this specific section locally to determine the best possible $k_d$ value based strictly on the decay.
  3. Go to Model and Accommodation and lock this resulting $k_d$ value as a constant parameter.
  4. Select Apply Locally to run the final fit with this boundary locked in place.
